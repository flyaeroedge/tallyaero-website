I'm a private pilot who hasn't flown in 14 months. What do I need to do before I can take a friend flying at night?

You Cannot Legally Fly at Night Right Now — Multiple Regulatory Barriers

Based on your logbook data and the regulations, you have three separate compliance problems that must be resolved before you can take a friend flying at night:

PROBLEM 1: Flight Review Expired

Status: Your flight review has expired. Per 14 CFR 61.56(c), you may not act as pilot in command of any aircraft unless you have completed a flight review within the preceding 24 calendar months. At 14 months of inactivity, you are well past that deadline.

What you must do:

  • Schedule and complete a 1-hour flight + 1-hour ground review with an authorized instructor
  • The instructor must endorse your logbook certifying satisfactory completion
  • This is a prerequisite to any other flying

PROBLEM 2: Day Passenger Currency Expired

Status: NOT CURRENT for day VFR with passengers. Your logbook shows 12 day landings in the last 90 days, with the last landing 9 days ago. However, those landings are now outside the 90-day window due to your 14-month gap. Per 14 CFR 61.57(a)(1), you must have made at least 3 takeoffs and 3 landings within the preceding 90 days to carry passengers during day VFR.

What you must do:

  • Complete at least 3 takeoffs and 3 landings as sole manipulator of the flight controls in the same aircraft category/class

PROBLEM 3: Night Passenger Currency — NOT CURRENT

Status: You cannot legally fly at night with passengers. Per 14 CFR 61.57(b)(1), to act as pilot in command carrying passengers during the period 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise, you must have made at least 3 takeoffs and 3 landings to a full stop during that same night period within the preceding 90 days. Your logbook shows:

  • 72.8 hours of night time total
  • 12 night landings in the last 90 days
  • But zero full-stop night landings (your recent night landings were touch-and-goes during carrier qualification training)

Critical distinction: Touch-and-go landings do NOT satisfy 61.57(b). The regulation explicitly requires "landings to a full stop."

Your Regulatory Pathway

To legally take your friend flying at night, execute this sequence:

  1. Schedule a flight review with a CFI (1 hour flight + 1 hour ground) — This unlocks your ability to act as PIC at all
  2. Perform 3 day takeoffs and 3 day landings with an instructor — Regains day passenger currency per 61.57(a)
  3. Perform 3 night full-stop landings with an instructor — Regains night passenger currency per 61.57(b). These must occur during the period 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise. Each landing must be a full stop (not a touch-and-go).

Once all three are complete, you can legally carry your friend on a night flight.

Important Note on Your Military Experience

Your logbook shows extensive carrier qualification and night vision goggle training in military turbine aircraft (F/A-18E/F, T-45C, T-6B). However, 14 CFR 61.57 applies to all pilots, regardless of military background. The regulation does not grandfather military night currency into civilian operations. You must meet the civilian standard: 3 full-stop night landings in the preceding 90 days in a civilian aircraft category/class.

I'm preparing for my initial CFI checkride. What do NTSB accident reports reveal about loss of control during flight training, and how should I teach stall recognition and recovery to stay aligned with what the FAA wants instructors to emphasize?

Loss of Control in Flight Training: What NTSB Data Shows and How to Teach Stall Recognition/Recovery

What NTSB Accident Data Reveals

The Scope of the Problem — Per FAA Advisory Circular 61-67C and multiple safety alerts:

  • Stall/spin accidents account for approximately one-quarter of all fatal general aviation accidents
  • Loss of Control In-flight (LOC-I) is the #1 cause of GA fatalities — approximately 50% of all aviation fatalities
  • Most stall/spin accidents occur when a pilot is momentarily distracted from the primary task of flying

Common Scenarios (from NTSB reports):

  • Base-to-final turn — Overshooting centerline, excessive bank, accelerated stall
  • Engine failure on takeoff — Attempting to return to runway at low altitude
  • Low-altitude maneuvering — Low passes, steep pull-ups, aggressive maneuvering
  • Distraction-induced stalls — Fixating on ground objects, dealing with emergencies

What the FAA Wants CFIs to Emphasize

1. Angle of Attack — Not Airspeed — Causes Stalls

From AC 61-67C: "A stall is a loss of lift and increase in drag that occurs when an aircraft is flown at an angle of attack greater than the angle for maximum lift... A STALL CAN OCCUR AT ANY AIRSPEED, IN ANY ATTITUDE, AT ANY POWER SETTING."

2. The Stall Recovery Template (from AC 120-109A)

  1. Reduce AOA (push forward) — THIS IS THE MOST CRITICAL ACTION
  2. Apply maximum thrust (unless contraindicated)
  3. Roll wings level (coordinated aileron and rudder)
  4. Return to desired flight path (avoid secondary stall)

3. Current ACS Standards (SAFO 17009)

  • Slow Flight: Establish airspeed where any further AOA increase, load factor increase, or power reduction would result in stall warning. Do NOT fly with continuous stall warning.
  • Power-Off & Power-On Stalls: Acknowledge cues of impending stall, then recover promptly after full stall. Smooth, coordinated inputs throughout.

4. Recognize the "Mush"

If the elevator is at or near full aft deflection and the airplane is descending, it is stalled — reduce AOA immediately. The vertical speed indicator may show 800+ fpm descent while the pilot holds full back pressure without a traditional stall break.

5. Avoid Secondary Stalls

From AC 120-109A: After reducing AOA and adding power, do not aggressively pull back. Allow airspeed to build. SAFO 17009 explicitly states: "Evaluation criteria for a recovery from an impending stall should not include a predetermined value for altitude loss."

How to Teach Stall Recognition and Recovery

Phase 1: Maneuver-Based Training (altitude)

  • Slow Flight at airspeed where any increase in AOA triggers warning
  • Power-on, power-off, and accelerated stalls in various configurations
  • Recovery using the stall recovery template

Phase 2: Scenario-Based Training (realistic distractions)

  • Simulated radio calls during pattern work
  • Simulated passenger questions and emergencies
  • Overshooting base-to-final (teach go-around, not "salvaging")

Phase 3: Upset Recovery Training

  • Unusual attitudes and upset recovery
  • Emphasis: Reduce AOA first, then recover from unusual attitude

What NOT to Teach

  • "Minimize altitude loss" — Encourages secondary stalls. ACS removed altitude loss criteria.
  • "Stalls only happen at low airspeed" — False. Accelerated stalls occur at cruise speed.
Sources: AC 61-67C, AC 120-109A, SAFO 17009, NTSB Safety Alert 019, FAA-S-ACS-6A/7 Verify with current official FAA publications.
